\section{Introduction}
A \squeeze{} is a paper cast taken from an inscribed stone surface. It is not an ink image. It is a
shallow relief surface, photographed under raking light, where a letter stroke can be clear in one
illumination direction and nearly absent in the other. Surface wear, paper grain, broken relief, and
the geometry of the light all decide what evidence is visible.

The deployed recognizer treats this as a physical multi-view reading problem. The visual backbone is
\model{}, a dual-light TrOCR line-recognizer operating on horizontal crops containing up to 4 characters (referred to as tile4 crops) that packs both raking-light scans into one RGB tensor.
The production transcript is then selected by a \charpost{} correction layer: a per-character posterior
lattice, train-only character language models, and a learned ridge row ranker. In short, the line model
supplies strong visual evidence; the lattice layer turns that evidence into letter-by-letter candidate
rows and chooses the row that is visually and linguistically most plausible.

The artifact tree is rooted at \texttt{data/}. It contains one all-train line recognizer,
five fold-excluded line recognizers, fold-clean character-posterior classifiers, character language
models, the OOF ridge ranker, and decode artifacts. The notebook
\texttt{greek\_squeezes.ipynb} validates or rebuilds the same tree locally, while \texttt{modal\_app.py}
builds and applies it on Modal.

The most recent external evaluation was run on
\texttt{papadimas/trogs-26-test-images}, split \texttt{test}. This separate dataset has 50 squeezes,
100 images, 605 scored rows, and 7,919 scored characters. The production pipeline scored
\cer{} \textbf{0.0822} (651 character errors).

\section{Data and Evaluation Hygiene}
The internal TROGS development data keeps both scans of a physical squeeze in the same split or fold.
This matters because \texttt{Rotation1} and \texttt{Rotation2} are two photographs of the same
inscription, not independent examples. The line recognizers are trained from official train
material only; the five OOF recognizers each exclude their held-out fold. Clean OOF means the held-out
fold is excluded from three places:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item the fold TrOCR checkpoint used to initialize visual evidence,
  \item the fold character-posterior classifier,
  \item the fold train-only character n-gram language models.
\end{enumerate}

Validation and internal test outputs are report-only once the recipe is frozen. External production
sets, including \texttt{trogs26-test}, are not used for training, checkpoint selection, ranker fitting,
or hyper-parameter choice.

\FloatBarrier
\section{Dual-Light Line Recognition}
\label{sec:duallight}
\subsection{Physical motivation}
Raking light is useful precisely because it is directional. A groove or ridge casts a shadow when the
light crosses it, but can flatten into the paper texture when the light runs along it. The two scans are
therefore complementary: one view may reveal a vertical stroke while the other reveals the crossbar or
the opposite ridge wall. Decoding the scans separately asks the recognizer to choose a photograph before
it has read the text. Dual-light packing instead gives the encoder both photographs at once.

\subsection{Input representation}
The line recognizer is the \model{} recipe: tile4 crops (horizontal crops containing up to 4 characters), a
\texttt{microsoft/trocr-large-printed} encoder-decoder, and dual-light RGB packing. The paired crops are
phase-registered and packed into the three channels that TrOCR already expects:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Channel} & \textbf{Content} \\
\midrule
R & \texttt{Rotation1} crop \\
G & \texttt{Rotation2} crop, phase-registered to \texttt{Rotation1} \\
B & pixelwise \texttt{min(R,G)}, a dark-stroke union image \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The third channel is the key visual trick. It is not a natural color image; it is a dark-stroke union.
If either lighting direction makes a groove dark, \texttt{min(R,G)} keeps that evidence dark. Training
also uses random R/G channel swap and mono dropout 0.15, so the model cannot rely on a fixed lighting
order or a single view. At evaluation time those stochastic augmentations are disabled, while phase
registration and \texttt{DUAL\_MODE=min} remain enabled.

\subsection{Training recipe}
The all-train line checkpoint at \texttt{data/line\_recognizer/all\_train} uses:

\begin{itemize}
  \item base model \texttt{microsoft/trocr-large-printed};
  \item processor base \texttt{microsoft/trocr-base-printed};
  \item real crops only, no synthetic data;
  \item tile4 crops of up to 4 characters (\texttt{lmc=4});
  \item 6000 optimizer steps, batch 96, gradient accumulation 1;
  \item learning rate \(2\times 10^{-5}\), gradient checkpointing, validation gate \(\cer{} < 0.20\);
  \item dual-light phase registration with max shift 0.15.
\end{itemize}

The fold checkpoints under \texttt{data/line\_recognizer/oof\_fold0} through
\texttt{oof\_fold4} use the same recipe, but each excludes the corresponding OOF fold.

The training objective is the standard TrOCR sequence-to-sequence cross-entropy: the decoder learns
to emit the proxy-character transcript of each packed tile4 crop. Note the division of labor at
deployment time, detailed in Section~\ref{sec:division}: the fine-tuned \emph{encoder} carries the
dual-light visual knowledge forward into the \charpost{} classifiers, while the TrOCR
\emph{decoder} itself serves as a reference model and is not part of the production decode path.

\section{The \charpost{} Correction Layer}
\label{sec:charpost}
The deployed correction layer turns the line recognizers into a per-character posterior pipeline.
It converts every transcript row into a \emph{lattice}: one probability distribution over proxy
characters per letter position. The row decoder can then separate two failure modes that are hard
to distinguish in a single line string: the correct row may be absent from the visual lattice, or it
may be present but ranked below a visually similar alternative. This section explains every trained
component and every decode-time step.

\subsection{Division of labor: what is trained, what runs at decode}
\label{sec:division}
A point that the pipeline diagram alone does not make obvious: \emph{at decode time the TrOCR
decoder is never run}. The production transcript is produced entirely by the \charpost{} lattice
machinery below; the dual-light line recognizers contribute their encoder weights, which initialize
the \charpost{} classifiers, and serve as reference/diagnostic models. Table~\ref{tab:components}
lists each component, its training data, and its role at decode.

\subsection{Alphabet and tile1 evidence}
The \charpost{} alphabet is explicit and fixed:
\texttt{ABCDEFGHIKLMNOPQRSTUWXYZ\&:)} --- the 24 Latin proxy letters used by the TROGS transcription
convention (no \texttt{J} or \texttt{V}) plus the three non-letter symbols that occur in the official
row transcripts, 27 classes in total. Training and decode both operate on \emph{tile1 crops}: one
dual-light crop per annotated letter box, built with exactly the same R/G/B packing, phase
registration, and \texttt{min(R,G)} union channel as the line recognizer
(Section~\ref{sec:duallight}). Crops are rendered once into a deterministic on-disk image cache
(keyed by split, squeeze base, row index, and box index) so that classifier training, logit caching,
and reproduction runs all see byte-identical inputs.

\subsection{Training the character-posterior classifier}
Each \charpost{} classifier is the ViT encoder of a line-recognizer checkpoint with a linear
classification head over the 27 classes on the encoder's \texttt{[CLS]} token. The frozen recipe:
initialize from the fold-excluded (or all-train) line recognizer, fine-tune encoder and head jointly
with cross-entropy for 3{,}000 optimizer steps at batch 96, learning rate $10^{-5}$, weight decay
$10^{-4}$, and inverse-square-root class weighting to protect rare symbols. The five OOF classifiers
exclude their held-out fold from training and validate on it; the all-train classifier uses all
official train material. This is where the line model's visual knowledge enters the deployed
decoder: the encoder has already learned dual-light stroke evidence from tile4 recognition, and the
tile1 fine-tune converts that into calibrated per-character posteriors.

\subsection{Posterior caching and the row lattice}
After training, the classifier is run once over every tile1 crop of the target split with fixed
five-view test-time augmentation (the center crop plus four $\pm 4$\,px translations, logits
averaged). The averaged logits are cached to compressed \texttt{.npz} artifacts keyed by squeeze
base, row index, and box index. At decode, cached logits are log-softmaxed, so a row with $n$ letter
boxes becomes the lattice
\[
  L \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 27}, \qquad
  L_{i,c} = \log P(\text{character } c \mid \text{crop } i),
\]
and every downstream step works only on $L$ --- no further GPU work is needed.

\subsection{Train-only character language models}
The LMs are interpolated character $n$-grams over the same 27-symbol alphabet, trained on official
train row transcripts only (fold-excluded copies for OOF work). With $\hat p_m$ the maximum-likelihood
estimate from $m$-gram counts, the model interpolates recursively from a uniform base:
\[
  p_0(c) = \tfrac{1}{27}, \qquad
  p_m(c \mid \mathrm{ctx}) = \lambda\, \hat p_m(c \mid \mathrm{ctx}) + (1-\lambda)\, p_{m-1}(c \mid \mathrm{ctx}_{m-1}),
  \qquad \lambda = 0.4 .
\]
Three orders are trained and used side by side: 6, 10, and 12. Short orders generalize across
formulaic epigraphic phrases; long orders can lock onto recurring full formulas. The ranker learns
how much to trust each.

\subsection{Candidate generation: beam search over the lattice}
Candidate rows are proposed by beam search over $L$. A prefix ending at position $i$ is extended by
each of the top-8 characters of position $i{+}1$ (char-top-k~8) and scored as
\[
  \mathrm{score}(t_{1..i+1}) \;=\; \mathrm{score}(t_{1..i}) \;+\; L_{i+1,\,c}
  \;+\; w \cdot \log p_{\mathrm{LM}}(c \mid t_{1..i}),
\]
keeping the best 256 prefixes per position (beam 256). The search runs once purely visually
($w{=}0$) and once for every combination of LM order $\{6,10,12\}$ and proposal weight
$w \in \{0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$; the resulting rows are pooled and deduplicated. Because the
beam emits exactly one character per lattice position, every candidate has the row's length ---
the pool differs only in \emph{which} character each position claims. A typical row yields a few
hundred distinct candidates.

\subsection{Row features and the ridge ranker}
Each pooled candidate $t$ is then scored against the \emph{full} (untruncated) lattice with seven
features:
\[
  \texttt{visual\_sum} = \sum_i L_{i,\,t_i}, \qquad
  \texttt{mean\_char\_logprob} = \tfrac{1}{|t|}\texttt{visual\_sum}, \qquad
  \texttt{length} = |t|,
\]
\[
  \texttt{length\_delta} = |t| - n, \qquad
  \texttt{lm}k\texttt{\_sum} = \textstyle\sum_i \log p_{\mathrm{LM}_k}(t_i \mid t_{<i}),
  \quad k \in \{6, 10, 12\}.
\]
\texttt{length\_delta} is candidate
length minus \emph{lattice} length, never candidate length minus ground-truth length, so the feature
cannot leak label information (with the current one-character-per-position beam it is identically
zero and is retained for future compatibility). The ranker itself is deliberately simple: features are
standardized and a ridge regression ($\alpha{=}1$) is fit to predict the \emph{negative edit
distance} of each candidate to the ground-truth row. At decode, the candidate with the highest
predicted score wins. Since \texttt{length} and \texttt{length\_delta} are constant within a row,
the within-row choice effectively reduces to a learned linear blend of visual and LM evidence; the
length features calibrate the regression target across rows of different sizes.

The ranker is fit only on \emph{clean OOF evidence}. For each fold, the held-out fold's rows are
decoded with the fold-excluded classifier (itself initialized from the fold-excluded line
recognizer) and fold-excluded LMs, producing a candidate table in which no model component has seen
the row being scored. Ranker quality is then evaluated with nested fold-heldout scoring --- fit on
four folds, evaluate on the fifth --- before a final fit on all OOF candidates is frozen for
production. The candidate table also yields an \emph{oracle} reference (always picking the
lowest-error candidate), which bounds how much any ranker could still gain from the same pool.

\subsection{A worked decode example}
\label{sec:worked}
Two figures at the end of this section show the mechanism end to end on the same real row, picked automatically
by the notebook from the cached all-train test decode: it scans rows for a case where the
ranker overturns the visual-only argmax and lands on the ground truth, which is exactly the
situation the layer exists to handle. The pattern that makes such a row worth showing is a
near-tie in the visual lattice at one or more positions --- two candidate characters within a
fraction of a nat of each other, the kind of margin a worn or ambiguous stroke produces --- while
every LM order and proposal weight in the beam search agrees on a different, linguistically far
more plausible row. Because the ranker's standardized ridge blend puts positive weight on both
\texttt{visual\_sum} and the LM sums (see the learned weights in Section~\ref{sec:results}), it
can accept a small visual deficit in exchange for a large gain in language-model plausibility.
Visual evidence proposes and constrains;
language evidence arbitrates near-ties; the fitted ranker decides, from OOF-fit weights, how much
each is worth. The exact row, its lattice, and its candidate scores are reproduced
in the two figures at the end of this section.

\subsection{The decode recipe, end to end}
At inference on a new squeeze, the pipeline therefore runs:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item build one dual-light tile1 crop per annotated letter box (R = Rotation1, G = registered
        Rotation2, B = \texttt{min(R,G)});
  \item run the all-train \charpost{} classifier with five-view TTA; cache averaged logits;
  \item log-softmax the row's logits into the lattice $L$;
  \item propose candidates: visual beam plus one beam per LM order and proposal weight
        $w \in \{0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ (beam 256, char-top-k 8); pool and deduplicate;
  \item compute the seven features for every candidate against the full lattice;
  \item score with the standardized ridge fit and select the argmax row;
  \item concatenate selected rows in row order into the squeeze transcript.
\end{enumerate}
After OOF fitting is complete, the all-train \charpost{} classifier and train-only LMs produce the
validation, internal test, and production decodes with exactly this recipe.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
Table~\ref{tab:results} records the \model{} line-model reference numbers, the deployed \charpost{}
results on the internal validation and test splits, and the external production result. The line-model
rows are useful diagnostics for the underlying visual backbone; the \charpost{} rows show the full
correction layer on the same internal splits; the external score is the production run described
above.

The ladder in Figure~\ref{fig:ladder} includes a production visual-only bar alongside the deployed
production ranker bar, making the comparison same-dataset.
That bar needs no separate production run: the \charpost{} decoder already builds each row's full lattice
before the ranker ever runs, so the visual-only argmax on \texttt{trogs26-test} is recovered directly
from the cached production logits used for the deployed decode. The frozen ranker closes only part of
the OOF gap between visual-only decoding and the lattice oracle --- \cer{} $0.0855\to 0.0516$ against
an oracle of $0.0114$ --- recovering about 46\% of the error that the same candidate pool could in
principle remove. The remaining 54\% is headroom for a stronger ranker or richer features on the same
lattice, not evidence that the lattice lacks the answer. The gap between the two production bars is the
same-dataset measure of how much of the ranker's OOF gain transfers to \texttt{trogs26-test}. The
external sample is still small enough (605 rows, 7,919 characters) that any of these \cer{} point
estimates carry a non-trivial confidence interval on their own.

\section{Reproducibility}
The notebook \texttt{greek\_squeezes.ipynb} validates or rebuilds the artifact tree under \texttt{data/}
and regenerates every figure in this report into \texttt{report/figs/} from whichever cached artifacts
are present, including the lattice graph and heatmap, the confusion matrix, the CER ladder, the
ranker weights, and the model-view figures. The same pipeline runs on Modal via \texttt{modal\_app.py},
which builds all artifacts from scratch and stages production decode outputs under
\texttt{data/prod\_runs/<slug>/}. Reusable artifacts are shared through the project Hugging Face

\section{Conclusion}
To read both raking-light views in a single forward pass, the system uses a registered RGB packing with a \texttt{min(R,G)} union channel. The deployed \charpost{} layer turns the line model into a clean OOF-trained character lattice and ridge row ranker. On the internal splits the full pipeline cuts the line model's \cer{} by 35--36\% (0.0358\,$\to$\,0.0229 on validation, 0.0320\,$\to$\,0.0209 on test); on the separate TROGS-26 test-image dataset it scores \cer{} \textbf{0.0822}. The pipeline is reproducible through \texttt{greek\_squeezes.ipynb}, cacheable under \texttt{data/}, and runnable on Modal.
